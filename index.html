🔬 AMINO ACIDS

General Formula: RCH(NH₂)COOH
Key Feature: Contains BOTH amine (-NH₂) AND carboxyl (-COOH) groups on the SAME carbon (α-amino acids)

Optical Isomerism

Remember: ALL amino acids show optical isomerism EXCEPT glycine (R = H, no chiral carbon)

Amphoteric Nature

⚠️ EXAM ESSENTIAL: Must show BOTH reactions!
With Acids (HCl) With Bases (NaOH)
NH₂ + H⁺ → NH₃⁺
Forms positive ion		 COOH + OH⁻ → COO⁻ + H₂O
Forms negative ion
🎯 Common Exam Question:
"Draw the structure of alanine at pH 2, pH 6 (pI), and pH 11"
Remember: Show correct charges on each group!

pH Effects

Isoelectric point: The pH at which the amino acid exists as a zwitterion / has no net charge
Zwitterion: A dipolar ion / has both a positive and a negative charge
pH < pI → BASE behavior
pH > pI → ACID behavior
pH = pI → ZWITTERION
(pI = isoelectric point)

🔗 PEPTIDES & PROTEINS

Peptide Formation:
Amino acid + Amino acid → Dipeptide + H₂O
Peptide link: -CONH- (amide link)
Remember: 2 amino acids → 2 different dipeptides possible!

Hydrolysis Methods

Method Conditions Products
Acid 6 mol dm⁻³ HCl
Reflux 24 hours		 NH₃⁺ ions
Alkaline Moderately conc. NaOH
Heat		 COO⁻ ions
🎯 Common Exam Question:
"Draw the products when a dipeptide is hydrolysed under acidic conditions"
Key: All amino acids will have NH₃⁺ groups!

Protein Structure Levels

1° Primary: Sequence (Gly-Ala-Phe)
2° Secondary: α-helix (3.6 aa/turn), β-sheet
3° Tertiary: 3D shape
4° Quaternary: Multiple chains
⚠️ Know ALL bonding types in tertiary structure:
  • Van der Waals (non-polar: Leu, Val, Phe)
  • H-bonds (polar: Ser -OH, Gln -CONH₂)
  • Ionic bonds (charged: Lys -NH₃⁺, Asp -COO⁻)
  • S-S bonds (ONLY between cysteines)
🎯 Common 6-Mark Question:
"Explain how the primary structure determines the tertiary structure"
Points needed: Sequence determines R groups → R groups interact → specific 3D shape → shape determines function

⚗️ ENZYMES

Enzyme: A biological catalyst / protein that speeds up the rate of a reaction
Substrate: The reactant in an enzyme-catalysed reaction / molecule that binds to the active site
Active site: Region with complementary shape to substrate
Stereospecificity: Only ONE enantiomer fits

Models

Lock & Key = Fixed shape
Induced Fit = Flexible shape

Inhibition

Competitive: Similar shape to substrate → competes for active site
Drug design: Many drugs work by enzyme inhibition
🎯 Common Exam Question:
"Explain why only one optical isomer of a drug may be effective"
Key: Active site is stereospecific → only one enantiomer has complementary shape

🧬 DNA STRUCTURE

Nucleotide Components

  1. Phosphate group (dihydrogen phosphate)
  2. Pentose sugar (2-deoxyribose)
  3. Organic base:
    • Purines: A, G (double ring)
    • Pyrimidines: C, T (single ring)
Base Pairing Rules:
A-T = 2 H-bonds
C-G = 3 H-bonds
"AT 2gether, CG has 3"
⚠️ Strands separate WITHOUT breaking covalent bonds!
(Only H-bonds between bases break)
🎯 Common Calculation:
"Calculate the number of H-bonds in a DNA fragment with 40% G-C base pairs"
Method: If 40% GC, then 60% AT. For 100 base pairs: (40 × 3) + (60 × 2) = 240 H-bonds

💊 CISPLATIN

[PtCl₂(NH₃)₂]
Square planar, CN = 4
Only cis-isomer is active!
(2 Cl⁻ ions next to each other)

Mechanism (4 steps)

  1. Hydrolysis: Cl⁻ → H₂O (inside cell)
  2. 1st substitution: Guanine N7 replaces H₂O
  3. 2nd substitution: Another guanine N7 bonds
  4. Result: Intrastrand crosslink → prevents replication → apoptosis
🎯 Common Exam Question:
"Explain why cisplatin can cause side effects"
Key points: Binds to DNA in healthy cells too → affects rapidly dividing cells (hair, blood, immune cells)
Side effects: Hair loss, immune suppression, anaemia
Why? Affects ALL rapidly dividing cells
